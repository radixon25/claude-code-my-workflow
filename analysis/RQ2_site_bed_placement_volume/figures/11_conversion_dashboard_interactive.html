What is the Conversion Coefficient?

Each shelter gets a Poisson regression estimating: “When this shelter’s available beds double, how does its placement rate change?”

  • Positive coefficient (green) = more open beds → more placements. The shelter efficiently converts available capacity into placements.
  • Near-zero coefficient = bed availability does not predict placements at this site. Other factors (approval speed, client readiness, staffing) may be the bottleneck.
  • Negative coefficient (red) = more open beds correlate with fewer placements. This is counterintuitive and usually reflects low overall volume or operational issues rather than a causal effect.

Beds are log-transformed to handle the wide range of shelter sizes. A coefficient of 0.50 means doubling available beds increases placements by about 41% (20.50 − 1 ≈ 0.41). The model also controls for system-wide demand (tbc_open_caseload).

Status Definitions

  • ✓ Reliable: Regression converged with a numerically stable estimate. Criteria: ≥ 5 placements, mean beds ≥ 1.0, bed variation (SD > 0.5), |coefficient| < 3, and SE < 2. Use these for operational decision-making.
  • Unreliable estimate: Regression converged but produced extreme or numerically unstable results—typically because the shelter has very few placements, low bed variance, or both. Do not use for comparisons.
  • Insufficient data: Fewer than 3 total placements or fewer than 14 site-days in the analysis period. No regression was attempted. Monitor as more data accumulates.

How Demographic is Determined

Each shelter is categorized by all populations it serves based on the city’s bed tracker data. We identify every distinct population label (Men, Women, Youth, Families) across all bed sub-sites for each shelter group.

  • Men & Women: Shelter operates beds for both men and women (e.g., YWLA has YWLA-Men + YWLA-Women sub-sites)
  • Men: Shelter operates beds exclusively for men
  • Women: Shelter operates beds exclusively for women
  • Women & Families: Shelter operates beds for women and families
  • Youth: Shelter operates beds for youth
  • Families: Shelter operates beds exclusively for families

This approach captures multi-population shelters accurately rather than assigning a single majority label.

Methodology

  • Data: TBC placements from Salesforce matched to city bed tracker via a curated crosswalk (90% coverage of eligible placed cases). EHARC and AIC excluded (not in TBC singles/families pipeline).
  • Model: Site-specific Poisson regression: placements ~ log(beds_lag1+1) + tbc_open_caseload + temperature + cold_day for each shelter independently.
  • Beds (log-transformed): Uses log(yesterday’s 9am bed count + 1) to handle the wide range of shelter sizes. Coefficient has an elasticity-like interpretation: doubling available beds → roughly 2β − 1 percent change in placements.
  • Demand control: Daily count of open TBC cases (tbc_open_caseload) controls for system-wide demand pressure on any given day.
  • Lag: Uses lag-1 (yesterday’s 9am beds) to predict today’s placements, reflecting the ~36-hour operational pipeline from bed availability to case closure in Salesforce.
  • Weather controls: Average temperature and cold-day indicator (min < 20°F) control for weather-driven demand variation.
  • Period: 2026-01-10 through 2026-02-23 (44 days).

Technical Notes
Conversion coefficients are estimated via site-specific Poisson regressions using glm(n_placed ~ log(beds_lag1+1) + tbc_open_caseload + avg_temp_f + cold_day, family=poisson). Beds are log-transformed for scale invariance; TBC open caseload controls for system demand. EHARC and AIC excluded (not in TBC singles/families pipeline). The system-level Poisson FE model (pooling all sites with shelter + day-of-week fixed effects) yields a pooled result (coef=0.33409, p=0.0019), but site-specific models reveal substantial heterogeneity across shelters.

Data pipeline: TBC work orders from Salesforce → parquet extract → crosswalk to city bed tracker site names → site-day panel (1406 rows, 32 shelters, 44 days).
